Organic Nomenclature

Alkanes are the most basic of organic chemicals. They include only carbons and hydrogens, where the carbons are all bonded together in a line and the hydrogens are bonded to them. Their names are dependent on how many carbons are in the chain. Here are the first nine with diagrams:
Methane: CH4
Ethane: CH3-CH3
Propane: CH3-CH2-CH3
Butane: CH3-CH2-CH2-CH3
Pentane: CH3-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH3
Hexane: CH3-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH3
Heptane: CH3-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH3
Octane: CH3-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH3
Nonane: CH3-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH2 -CH2-CH2-CH3
Alkenes are slightly more advanced than alkanes. The functional group of an alkene is a Carbon-Carbon double bond(C=C). Names for alkenes are the same as for alkanes, but have -ene in place of -ane. If the molecule can have a double bond in multiple places, a number is required before the -ene to show where the bond is. The number should be the number of the carbon that the bond is on closest to the outside. Here are two examples:
Ethene: CH2=CH2 Here there is no number required because the C=C bond can only be on the outer carbons.
But-2-ene: CH3-CH=CH-CH3 A number is required here because the C=C bond could either be between the first and second carbons or the second and third carbons. Since the bond is between carbons 2 and 3 and 2 is less than 3, we use 2 in the name. This principle of numbering is used for other organic molecules, so it would be helpful to remember it.
Alkynes are the same as Alkenes but with a Carbon-Carbon triple(C≡C) bond instead of double. They use the -yne ending. Here are some examples:
Ethyne: CH≡CH
But-2-yne: CH3-C≡C-CH3
Alcohols are different from the other organic molecules discussed so far. Instead of having a different carbon-carbon bond, they have a functional group. This group is OH, an oxygen bonded to a hydrogen. The numbering, like before, is based on the carbon the functional group is attached to. The ending, -ol, replaces the 'e' at the end of the alkane names. Here are some examples:
Ethanol: CH3-CH2-OH
Butan-2-ol: CH3-CH2-CH(OH)-CH3
Aldehydes always have a carbon-oxygen double bond(C=O) at one of their ends. No number is required for naming because the C=O bond is always at the end. The ending is -al and replaces the 'e' at the end. Here are some examples:
Ethanal: CH3-CH=OH
Butanal: CH3-CH2-CH2-CH=O
Ketones are like aldehydes, but their C=O bond is not on one of the end carbons. This means numbering is required in most cases. The ending is -one, which replaces the 'e'. Here are some examples:
Propanone: CH3-CO-CH3
Butan-2-one: CH3-CO-CH2-CH3
Carboxylic Acids are when one of the end carbons is bonded to both an OH group and double bonded to an oxygen. Numbering is not required since it is always an end carbon. The ending is -oic acid, which replaces the 'e'. Here are some examples:
Ethanoic acid: CH3-COOH
Butanoic acid: CH3-CH2 -CH2-COOH
