Organic Nomenclature

Alkanes are the most basic of organic chemicals. They include only carbons and hydrogens, where the carbons are all bonded together in a line and the hydrogens are bonded to them. Their names are dependent on how many carbons are in the chain. Here are the first nine with diagrams:
Methane: CH4
Ethane: CH3-CH3
Propane: CH3-CH2-CH3
Butane: CH3-CH2-CH2-CH3
Pentane: CH3-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH3
Hexane: CH3-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH3
Heptane: CH3-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH3
Octane: CH3-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH3
Nonane: CH3-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH2 -CH2-CH2-CH3
Alkenes are slightly more advanced than alkanes. The functional group of an alkene is a Carbon-Carbon double bond(C=C). Names for alkenes are the same as for alkanes, but have -ene in place of -ane. If the molecule can have a double bond in multiple places, a number is required before the -ene to show where the bond is. The number should be the number of the carbon that the bond is on closest to the outsid. Here are two examples:
Ethene: CH2=CH2 Here there is no number required because the C=C bond can only be on the outer carbons.
Meth-2-ene: CH3-CH=CH-CH3 A number is required here because the C=C bond could either ber between the first and second carbons or the second and third carbons. Since the bond is between carbons 2 and 3 and 2 is less than 3, we use 2 in the name.
